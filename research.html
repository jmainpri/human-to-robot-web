Research

Our research tries to uncover the principles of human and robot motion. In this context, we develop artificial intelligence and machine learning techniques able to implement motion skills and transfer behaviors from humans to robots. Our main application domains are service and collaborative robotics for which the notion of socially appropriate motion is key.



Human-Robot Manipulation

We design robotic software frameworks that account explicitly for humans in close proximity by introducing predictive and mimicking capacities.

Human Movement Analysis

We elaborate machine learning techniques to extract behavioral models from data arising from motion capture of humans.

Motion Planning

We devise algorithms to autonomously generate robotic motions in geometrically complex, uncertain and dynamic workspaces.

Inverse Optimal Control

Inverse Optimal Control (IOC/IRL) has been studied for more that 15 years and is of fundamental importance in robotics. It allows learning a utility function "explaining'' the behavior of an agent, and can thus be used for imitation or prediction of a given behavior by having solely access to demonstrated optimal or near optimal solutions.

In the context of learning manipulation behaviors from demonstration and characterizing human motion principles, we are investigating IOC algorithms that can construct utility functions from low-level features. These stem from sensory feeds such as visual or tactile sensor feeds, which are inherently high-dimensional. Thus the problems we consider are continuous in state and action, with high-dimensional action spaces and very high-dimensional feature spaces. In this project we investigate using deep function approximators to model the utility functions, for which Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are particular well suited, especially for modeling utility in the workspace geometry.

Motion Optimization

Motion optimization is a special brand of motion planning techniques which generally consists of continuously bending the trajectory into a better solution. Motion optimization are generally viewed as local methods as they seek to ameliorate the solution by leveraging the gradient of the objective function, and thus they typically suffer from getting trapped in local minima.

However, by pulling-back a model of the environment in the configuration space of the robot (in the Riemannian sense), it is now possible to solve motion planning problems with motion optimization for 7DoFs manipulators in cluttered workspaces within the order of a second. We are working in collaboration with Lula Robotics on algorithms that allow to account for arbitrary geometries as perceived by real world sensors.

Human-Robot Collaboration

Humans exhibit incredible manipulation capabilities in terms of reactivity and coordination regarding other humans in the environment. Achieving such human level performances with robots requires acquiring predictive models of human behavior, while constantly integrating high-dimensional sensor feeds at multiple time scales.

Conceptualizing such control architectures, able to account explicitly for the human partner is both challenging and of fundamental importance in robotics because it attempts to identify and implement the subtlety of what humans expect from robot motion when in close proximity. This project aims to characterize and evaluate empirically "human-aware" control architectures by introducing human evaluation in the loop.

Human Movement Understanding

Although the underlying principles of human motion have been investigated in terms of muscle activation and neural activity, we have little understanding of full-body motion generation in dynamic, cluttered environments and especially in presence of social and physical interaction.

Understanding human motion requires the collaboration of researchers from many fields of science (e.g., cognitive science, neuro-science, bio-mechanics). A lot of research has sought to understand human motion amidst obstacles. However, no global mathematical theory exists describing how the different sensor streams are consumed by the central nervous system to generate behavior. This project primarily aims at understanding how humans model their surroundings, in terms of static geometry or other humans, by making use of inverse optimal control as the primary technique of investigation.
